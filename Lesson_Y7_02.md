## Part 1: Fractions — Structure and Operations

### What a Fraction Means
A fraction \(\dfrac{a}{b}\) means \(a\) parts out of \(b\) equal parts.

- \(a\) is the **numerator** (how many parts you have)
- \(b\) is the **denominator** (how many equal parts the whole is divided into)
- \(b \neq 0\) always — dividing by zero is undefined

**Equivalent fractions:** Multiply or divide numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number:
\[\frac{2}{3} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{10}{15}\]

**Simplest form (lowest terms):** Divide both by their HCF (Highest Common Factor).
\[\frac{24}{36} = \frac{24 \div 12}{36 \div 12} = \frac{2}{3}\]

---

### Adding and Subtracting Fractions

**Same denominator:** Add/subtract numerators only.
\[\frac{3}{7} + \frac{2}{7} = \frac{5}{7}\]

**Different denominators:** Find the LCM (Lowest Common Multiple) of the denominators, convert both fractions, then add.

**Worked Example 1:** Calculate \(\dfrac{3}{4} + \dfrac{5}{6}\).

LCM of 4 and 6 is 12:
\[\frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{12}, \quad \frac{5}{6} = \frac{10}{12}\]
\[\frac{9}{12} + \frac{10}{12} = \frac{19}{12} = 1\frac{7}{12}\]

**Worked Example 2:** Calculate \(2\dfrac{1}{3} - \dfrac{3}{4}\).

Convert to improper fractions first:
\[2\frac{1}{3} = \frac{7}{3}\]
LCM of 3 and 4 is 12:
\[\frac{7}{3} = \frac{28}{12}, \quad \frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{12}\]
\[\frac{28}{12} - \frac{9}{12} = \frac{19}{12} = 1\frac{7}{12}\]

---

### Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

**Multiplying:** Multiply numerators together, multiply denominators together. Simplify before or after.
\[\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{8}{9} = \frac{3 \times 8}{4 \times 9} = \frac{24}{36} = \frac{2}{3}\]

*Tip: Cancel common factors before multiplying (cross-cancellation):*
\[\frac{\cancel{3}^1}{4} \times \frac{8}{\cancel{9}^3} = \frac{1 \times 8}{4 \times 3} = \frac{8}{12} = \frac{2}{3}\]

**Dividing:** Multiply by the **reciprocal** of the divisor (flip the second fraction).
\[\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}\]

**Worked Example 3:** Calculate \(\dfrac{5}{6} \div \dfrac{2}{3}\).

\[\frac{5}{6} \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{15}{12} = \frac{5}{4} = 1\frac{1}{4}\]

**Worked Example 4:** Calculate \(1\dfrac{1}{2} \div 2\dfrac{1}{4}\).

Convert to improper fractions:
\[1\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{2}, \quad 2\frac{1}{4} = \frac{9}{4}\]
\[\frac{3}{2} \div \frac{9}{4} = \frac{3}{2} \times \frac{4}{9} = \frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}\]

## Part 2: Decimals and Place Value

### Place Value
Every digit has a position with a value that is a power of 10:

\[4\underbrace{7}_{1}\underbrace{3}_{0}\underbrace{.}_{}\underbrace{2}_{-1}\underbrace{5}_{-2}\underbrace{6}_{-3}\]

The digit 7 is in the **tens** place: value = \(7 \times 10 = 70\).
The digit 2 is in the **tenths** place: value = \(2 \times \frac{1}{10} = 0.2\).

### Rounding
To round to \(n\) decimal places:
1. Look at the \((n+1)\)th decimal digit
2. If it is \(\geq 5\), round up; if \(< 5\), round down (truncate)

**Worked Example 5:** Round \(3.7648\) to 2 decimal places.

The third decimal place is 4 (< 5), so round down: \(3.76\).

### Recurring Decimals
Some fractions produce decimals that repeat forever:
\[\frac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3} = 0.3333\ldots\]
\[\frac{2}{7} = 0.\overline{285714}\]

## Part 3: Percentages

### Converting Between Forms

| From | To | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Fraction | Percentage | Multiply by 100 |
| Percentage | Fraction | Divide by 100, simplify |
| Decimal | Percentage | Multiply by 100 |
| Percentage | Decimal | Divide by 100 |

**Examples:**
\[\frac{3}{4} = 0.75 = 75\%\]
\[12.5\% = \frac{12.5}{100} = \frac{1}{8} = 0.125\]

### Percentage of a Quantity
\[\text{Percentage of quantity} = \frac{\text{percentage}}{100} \times \text{quantity}\]

**Worked Example 6:** Find 35% of 240.
\[\frac{35}{100} \times 240 = 0.35 \times 240 = 84\]

### Percentage Increase and Decrease
\[\text{Percentage change} = \frac{\text{change}}{\text{original}} \times 100\%\]

**Worked Example 7:** A jacket costs \$80 and is on sale for \$68. Find the percentage discount.
\[\text{Change} = 80 - 68 = 12\]
\[\text{Percentage discount} = \frac{12}{80} \times 100 = 15\%\]

### Finding the Original Value (Reverse Percentage)
If a value after a 20% increase is \$120, find the original:
\[\text{Original} \times 1.20 = 120\]
\[\text{Original} = \frac{120}{1.20} = 100\]

**Worked Example 8:** After a 15% price reduction, a TV costs \$340. What was the original price?
\[\text{Original} \times 0.85 = 340\]
\[\text{Original} = \frac{340}{0.85} = \$400\]

## Part 5: Non-Routine Problem Block

1. **Fraction puzzle:** I think of a number. I add \(\dfrac{3}{4}\) to it, then multiply by \(\dfrac{2}{3}\), and get \(1\). What is the number?

2. **Who ran further?** Alex ran \(2\dfrac{3}{4}\) km. Jordan ran \(\dfrac{7}{3}\) km. By how much did the further runner exceed the other? Give your answer as a fraction and as a decimal.

3. **Percentage chain:** A laptop is discounted by 20%, then the sale price is discounted by a further 10%. What is the total percentage discount on the original price? (Is it 30%? Prove your answer.)

4. **Ordering challenge:** Arrange these in ascending order:
\[\frac{5}{7}, \quad 0.72, \quad 71\%, \quad \frac{11}{15}\]
Justify your ordering method.
