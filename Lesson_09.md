# Lesson 09 (Elite Track)

## Program Stage
- Year: 10 (accelerated stream)
- Term: 1
- Week: 3
- Session: Lesson 9
- Duration: 100 minutes

## Lesson Title
Sequences, Series, and Algebraic Pattern Proof

## Lesson Purpose
Develop structural pattern recognition and proof-ready sequence reasoning for accelerated algebra and specialist transition.

## Learning Intentions
By the end of this lesson, the student will:
1. Generate and analyse arithmetic and geometric sequences.
2. Use nth-term forms and partial-sum formulas correctly.
3. Solve sequence parameter problems.
4. Prove pattern claims with clear algebraic argument.

## Success Criteria
- Core set: `>= 85%`
- Challenge set: `>= 70%`
- Timed section completed with symbolic accuracy
- At least one proof-quality justification in full sentences

---

## Minute-by-Minute Flow (100 min)

### 0-10 min: Retrieval Warm-Up
1. 10th term of arithmetic sequence 4, 7, 10, ...
2. 6th term of geometric sequence 3, 6, 12, ...
3. Sum of first 10 natural numbers
4. Define common difference and common ratio
5. Quick simplification of sigma notation

### 10-30 min: Explicit Teaching
- Arithmetic: \(u_n=a+(n-1)d\), \(S_n=\dfrac{n}{2}[2a+(n-1)d]\).
- Geometric: \(u_n=ar^{n-1}\), \(S_n=a\dfrac{r^n-1}{r-1}\), \(r\neq1\).
- Pattern-proof structure: conjecture, algebra, conclusion.

### 30-55 min: Guided Core Set
1. Find \(u_{20}\) for arithmetic sequence \(a=5,d=4\).
2. Find \(S_{25}\) for same sequence.
3. For geometric sequence \(a=2,r=3\), find \(u_7\).
4. Find \(S_6\) for Question 3.
5. If arithmetic sequence has \(u_3=11, u_{10}=39\), find \(a,d\).
6. Prove sum of first n odd numbers is \(n^2\).

### 55-70 min: Singapore-Style Non-Routine Block
1. If \(u_n=3n+2\), find least n such that \(u_n>200\).
2. Sequence defined by \(u_{n+1}=2u_n+1, u_1=1\): compute first 6 terms and conjecture formula.
3. Show arithmetic mean of first and last term equals average term value in arithmetic sequence.

### 70-85 min: Timed ATAR-Style Mini Test
1. Arithmetic sequence with \(a=12,d=-3\): find first term below zero.
2. Geometric sequence with \(a=81,r=\dfrac13\): find least n with \(u_n<1\).
3. Find \(S_{12}\) if \(u_n=4n-1\).

### 85-100 min: Exit Ticket
1. Compute \(u_{15}\) for \(u_n=2n+5\).
2. Compute \(S_8\) for arithmetic sequence \(3,7,11,...\).
3. One sentence: when geometric models are more appropriate than arithmetic.

---

## Core Solutions
1. \(u_{20}=5+19(4)=81\)
2. \(S_{25}=\dfrac{25}{2}[10+24\cdot4]=1325\)
3. \(u_7=2\cdot3^6=1458\)
4. \(S_6=2\dfrac{3^6-1}{3-1}=728\)
5. \(a+2d=11, a+9d=39\Rightarrow d=4, a=3\)
6. \(1+3+...+(2n-1)=n^2\) by induction or pairing argument.

## Timed Test Solutions
1. \(12+(n-1)(-3)<0\Rightarrow n>5\), first below zero at \(n=6\)
2. \(81\left(\dfrac13\right)^{n-1}<1\Rightarrow 3^{5-n}<1\Rightarrow n>5\), least \(n=6\)
3. \(S_{12}=\sum_{n=1}^{12}(4n-1)=4\cdot\dfrac{12\cdot13}{2}-12=300\)

## Exit Ticket Answers
1. 35
2. 136
3. When growth is multiplicative by constant factor.

## Homework

### Core
1. Find \(u_{30}\) for arithmetic sequence 2, 9, 16, ...
2. Find \(S_{30}\) for same sequence
3. Find \(u_8\) and \(S_8\) for geometric sequence 5, 10, 20, ...
4. If \(u_n=7-2n\), find n for \(u_n=-53\)
5. Prove \(2+4+...+2n=n(n+1)\)

### Extension
6. Derive formula for \(\sum_{k=1}^n k^2\).
7. Recurrence \(u_{n+1}=u_n+2n+1\): connect to square numbers.
